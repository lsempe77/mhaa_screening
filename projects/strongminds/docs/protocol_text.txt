The review answers 16 prespecified research questions (RQ1–RQ16) covering drivers of depression, active intervention ingredients, dose, delivery format, workforce, group size, stepped-care architecture, durability, engagement, and cost. The review will use existing systematic reviews as a gateway to the evidence and do a deeper dive of relevant primary studies. We will synthesize the evidence through two streams. Stream 1 is anchored to the umbrella review by Barbui et al. (2020) and uses the included systematic reviews that meet our adult common-mental-disorder PICOS as the starting point.  We will exclude out-of-scope populations (children, refugees, and conditions beyond CMD) from Barbui et al. (2020). We will also run a targeted update search for systematic reviews published from 2019 onwards, applying the same criteria.Stream 2 (seed-paper-led) addresses the questions that Barbui et al. (2020) do not cover: determinants, dose-response, spillover, and stepped care. It anchors on named-seed reviews, expands via one-hop citation chasing, and then uses the resulting reviews to identify relevant primary studies, as in Stream 1. To stay within the rapid-review envelope, we will bound primary-study extraction to the questions that drive Minimum Viable Product (MVP) design. We will answer the remaining questions at the review level. In parallel, we will use internal StrongMinds evidence to triangulate findings from our review. Screening and extraction use a single-reviewer workflow with an LLM second screener and review by core team members. We will appraise the systematic reviews used as entry points with AMSTAR-2 in a rapid critical-domains mode. We will synthesize findings narratively by question with a GRADE-lite confidence rating, and produce a short evidence report that informs downstream milestones. Table of contentsPlain-language summary	71. To do so, the design stage needs a concise but defensible, rigorous summary of the existing evidence on what works, for whom, at what dose, who delivers it, and at what cost. This protocol outlines how we will assemble, screen, extract, and synthesise that evidence over an eight-week period. It combines external peer-reviewed literature with StrongMinds' own internal study findings. 1. IntroductionStrongMinds aims to reach 100 million people by 2035 with clinically validated, government-adoptable depression care delivered at approximately USD 1 per client. This goal requires a fundamental redesign of how individuals enter and progress through the care system. Non-specialists will deliver brief, structured, task-shifted interventions within a stepped-care architecture.The Phase 3 pilot program (October 2025 – April 2026) produced directional signals on three points. Psychoeducation (PE) and problem-solving (PS) emerged as the most promising active ingredients. Multi-session interventions outperformed single-session interventions at the 3-month follow-up. The minimum effective dose appeared to be two sessions. However, Phase 3 was non-randomized. It used a single Mental Health Counsellor per ingredient arm and produced effect estimates with wide uncertainty bands. The Phase 4 academic workstream therefore triangulates these internal signals against the wider evidence base before the team commits to a Minimum Viable Product (MVP) specification. This rapid review (Milestone 1, M1) serves as the foundation for that triangulation. It covers the published evidence on low-intensity, task-shifted, group-based depression interventions globally. The review is organized around 16 pre-specified research questions and integrates StrongMinds' internal evidence as a parallel stream. Its purpose is to:Establish what is known and not known across the design dimensions of the ULCM (active ingredients, dose, delivery, workforce, group size, severity stratification, durability, cost);Identify points of convergence and divergence between Phase 3 findings and external evidence;Recommend a candidate MVP design space, with explicit residual uncertainties to be resolved in Phase 5;Feed directly into Milestone 2 (decision-ready evidence pack) and Milestone 3 (integration workshop with the HCD workstream).The review is rapid by design. It is not a full systematic review and does not produce meta-analytic effect estimates. The structure remains transparent, reproducible, and defensible for a strategic decision-making audience (the StrongMinds Global Leadership Team, GLT). It works within the constraints of a single screener and an eight-week timeline.2. MethodsWe follow the rapid review principles set out by the Cochrane Rapid Reviews Methods Group (Garritty et al., 2021) and report according to PRISMA 2020 (Page et al., 2021), the PRISMA-S search reporting extension (Rethlefsen et al., 2021), and the PRISMA-RR rapid review reporting extension (Stevens et al., 2024). Section 7 details each methodological compromise we make relative to a full systematic review, in particular, single screening with LLM-assisted second screening. Cochrane RR guidance (Garritty et al., 2021) allows single-reviewer screening, with verification of excluded records by a second human reviewer. Our workflow replaces the second human with an LLM during the bulk screening stage. We validate that substitution against a pre-screened human gold-standard calibration set (see Appendix F.6 ). A core team member still handles (i) the calibration set itself and (ii) senior adjudication of disagreements and borderline decisions. This approach augments Cochrane RR guidance rather than implementing it directly. Given the eight-week timeline and its operational, decision-support purpose, we do not register the review with PROSPERO. This protocol serves as the preregistration record. 4.1 Eligibility criteriaStudies qualify if they meet all PICOST criteria in Table 6. In both streams, we work downwards from the reviews to the primary studies they contain. We use systematic reviews to locate and bound the relevant primary evidence efficiently within an eight-week window, in line with Cochrane Rapid Reviews guidance (Garritty et al., 2021). We appraise the systematic reviews using AMSTAR-2 and extend that appraisal to their primary studies.  We answer each research question through one of two evidence streams. The choice depends on whether the question sits inside the scope of the Barbui et al. (2020) umbrella review. Stream 1 — The Barbui et al. (2020) umbrella spans populations and conditions wider than our PICOS. It includes children, refugee and conflict-affected samples, and conditions beyond common mental disorders (CMD). For Stream 1, we (i) select the systematic reviews in Barbui et al. (2020) that meet our adult-CMD PICOS and follow them to their primary studies, extracting data from only the primary studies that meet our PICOS. We also (ii) run a targeted update search for systematic reviews published from 2019 onwards, narrowed to adult CMD and excluding child, perinatal, adolescent, and refugee or humanitarian samples. We likewise follow those reviews to their primary studies. The term set is lightly extended for specific questions (e.g., scalability, supervision, cost). This approach anchors the review on an established, high-quality umbrella while keeping the evidence current and on scope. Stream 1 answers the core intervention questions (RQ2, RQ3, RQ5, RQ6, RQ9, RQ10, RQ14) plus, with light extensions, facilitator model (RQ4), non-specialist delivery (RQ13), engagement (RQ15), and cost (RQ16).Stream 2 — seed-paper-led. Barbui et al. (2020) is a treatment-efficacy umbrella spanning mixed mental-health conditions. It does not cover the determinants of depression, dose-response, spillover to non-cases, or stepped-care architecture. Therefore, re-running its strings would not reveal the type of evidence we are seeking. For these questions, we therefore anchor on a small set of directly relevant systematic reviews (‘seeds’) and build outward by one-hop backwards and forward citation chasing, restricted to SR/MA for the entry-point set. We then follow the resulting reviews to their primary studies, as in Stream 1, and extract only those that meet the PICOS. Stream 2 answers RQ1 (drivers), RQ7 and RQ8 (dose and temporal effects), RQ11 (spillover), and RQ12 (stepped care). The named seeds per question are given in Table 2 to Table 5.4.1.2 Scope control Following the systematic reviews to their primary studies adds depth, but carries the risk of scope creep. We therefore bound the follow-up to keep the review tractable: Capped RQ set: We perform primary-study extraction only for the questions that need per-study granularity for MVP design: the nine headline RQs (RQ5, RQ6, RQ7, RQ8, RQ9, RQ12, RQ13, RQ14, RQ16) and RQ10 (group size).We answer the remaining questions (RQ1, RQ2, RQ3, RQ4, RQ11, RQ15) at review level. We focus only on each review’s pooled or narrative result, without drilling into its primary studies. One sourcing review per question: For each in-scope RQ, we draw primary studies from the most recent, highest-AMSTAR-2 review(s) for that question. A primary study cited by more than one review is extracted once and tagged with all sourcing reviews to avoid re-extracting the same trial. Effect/dose/cost focus: Within the capped set, we reserve full-schema extraction for primary studies that feed the effect-size, dose, and cost tabulations (Appendices D.5 and D.7). Other in-scope studies receive a reduced record (design, N, dose, facilitator, geography, headline effect). This approach bounds the primary study workload to the decisions it actually informs. Every other question remains answerable from its entry-point reviews.We prioritize Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) evidence in synthesis weighting for context-sensitive questions (RQ1, RQ4, RQ13). The dose-response, single-session, and stepped-care questions (RQ7, RQ8, RQ9, RQ12, RQ14) draw on reviews dominated by high-income-country (HIC) evidence (Cuijpers et al., 2013, 2019), as that is where the strongest dose- and SSI-review evidence is concentrated. This weighting comes in only at the synthesis stage. We don't use it to structure the search itself, and the protocol doesn't set up any geographic tiers.4.2 Search strategyWe use two parallel streams and combine the results into a single deduplicated corpus. We record the search end date and re-run the same strings as an update search in week 7, as standard practice. We extract any new records that meet PICOS criteria and integrate them before the final synthesis.4.3 ScreeningWe import all records (database hits and citation-chasing yield) to EPPI-Reviewer (Thomas et al., 2023) for deduplication, screening, and extraction management. We perform a second pass of deduplication in R using the synthesisr package, based on DOIs and normalized titles.4.3.1 Title and abstract screeningSingle-reviewer screening, augmented by LLM second-screening:Human pass: A single reviewer screens each record's title and abstract against the PICOST criteria and assigns one of three statuses: include, exclude, or undecided. Exclusions are tagged with a reason code following a set hierarchy — population, then design, intervention, outcome, context, and finally language or date.LLM pass: At the same time, we run each record through an LLM (Claude 4.7 or equivalent). The model gets the same PICOST criteria and the record's title and abstract, and returns three things: a decision, the first criterion the record failed on (if excluded), and a direct quote from the abstract that supports the decision.We screen each record three times, using paraphrased versions of the prompt. The number of times the three runs agree (from 0/3 to 3/3) gives us a confidence score for that record. To ensure the supporting quote is accurate, we check it against the source using an exact string match. If it does not match, we re-prompt the model.Adjudication: All disagreements between humans and LLMs, all human “undecided” decisions, and any LLM low-confidence cases (k = 1/3 or 2/3) are reviewed by the screening reviewer for final consensus. Audit. The LLM reviews a 10% random audit of human-excluded records as a missed-criterion check; flagged items go to the core team reviewer.Prompt performance is pre-calibrated against a held-out human gold standard before live screening (see Appendix F.6): 100 records (60 T/A, 40 full-text) are double-screened by the primary reviewer (L. Sempe) and the senior reviewer  acting as second human for the calibration set only, and LLM prompts are iterated until agreement with the human gold standard reaches Cohen’s κ ≥ 0.7 (T/A) and ≥ 0.6 (full-text), or the protocol is amended with a documented rationale. After calibration, live-screening κ is reported on a rolling 50-record window for drift monitoring. We do not treat live-corpus κ as a primary performance metric, because the LLM and the primary reviewer are not blinded to each other’s prior decisions during live screening; the calibration κ is the performance metric of record. Calibration curve metrics (ECE, Brier) (Marion et al., 2026) are not computed for this rapid review.4.3.2 Full-text screeningFull texts are retrieved through institutional access and direct contact with the authors. Records for which full text cannot be obtained after two rounds of requests are recorded as “not retrievable” and reported in the PRISMA flow diagram.Single-reviewer human screening with LLM cross-check:Human reviewer applies the full PICOST and assigns inclusion/exclusion with hierarchical reason code, plus additional codes assessable only at full text (e.g., adult subgroup separately reported; specific facilitator type; outcome measure used).The LLM, prompted with the full text segmented into abstract, methods, results, and discussion, returns a decision, exclusion code (if any), and verbatim supporting spans from the methods and results sections. Spans drawn only from the abstract are rejected and re-prompted. k = 3 runs per record.All human–LLM disagreements and all human-included records proceed to the screening core team reviewer for confirmation. All LLM-exclude decisions at full-text are audited by a human (asymmetric audit: false exclusions at full-text are the costlier error).4.3.3 Linked-publication groupingWhere multiple publications report on the same study (e.g., trial protocol, main results paper, secondary outcomes paper, cost-effectiveness paper), the LLM is used to cluster linked publications. The most recent or most complete publication is designated as the index record; companion publications are linked in the database, and their unique information (e.g., cost analyses published separately) is extracted into the index record’s extraction sheet.4.4 Data extractionWe perform extraction in EPPI-Reviewer using a structured template piloted on five studies before full deployment. This retains the verification rigor of the HSF mapping protocol (Marion et al., 2026) at the point where it matters most.Categorical and free-text fields are extracted by the LLM and audited by the human reviewer on a 20% random sample.Fields not extractable from the source are recorded as “not reported” with the location of the absence noted.4.4.2 Internal evidence streamWe extract StrongMinds' internal evidence into a parallel sheet using the same template as the external studies. This includes the Phase 1 active-ingredients work, Phase 2 dose-response, Phase 3a 18-arm pilot, Phase 3b triangulation report, the SSI report, the peer-to-peer study, and the Phase 4 HCD report (Acam & Hopkins, 2026). For this internal stream, we replace the risk-of-bias domain with a free-text "internal study caveats" field that flags design limitations such as single MHC per arm, non-randomized designs, and small sample sizes.Since the internal evidence base is not peer-reviewed, we use it for triangulation alongside the external corpus rather than pooling it.4.5 SynthesisThe review does not produce pooled meta-analytic estimates. The synthesis is narrative, organized by workstream, and structured around research questions. We use three complementary approaches, which are described below. 4.5.1 Vote-counting and harvest plotsFor each research question in which more than three studies report comparable directional outcomes, we present a harvest plot (Ogilvie et al., 2008) showing review counts by effect direction, stratified by evidence stream and, where relevant, by the predominant geography of the contributing reviews (SSA / other LMIC / HIC). Vote counts are reported with explicit acknowledgement of their limitations and are not used as the sole basis for a recommendation. The consistency rule that maps vote-counts to GRADE-lite confidence judgements is pre-specified: ≥ 75% of reviews in the same direction = consistent; 50–74% = mixed; < 50% = inconsistent. Direction is judged against the pre-registered direction of effect (favors intervention) and not re-derived post hoc.4.5.2 Targeted effect-size tabulationFor questions on dose, ingredient and group size (RQ5, RQ7, RQ10), we will report effect sizes per study. If we find more than 5 studies with similar study design and comparators, we present a forest plot without pooling, to show the distribution of effects. We describe heterogeneity qualitatively (study, population, intervention).4.5.3 Cost synthesisWe standardize all cost figures to 2026 USD. We first apply local-country consumer price index (CPI) inflation from the year of costing through 2026, sourced from the World Bank's WDI. We then apply a market exchange rate conversion to USD, using the IMF International Financial Statistics annual average for the year of costing. We restrict the cost perspective to the provider perspective. We record societal-perspective costs when reported, but do not use them as the headline figure. If the studies report only the provider sub-component of a societal-perspective figure, we use that sub-component. The cost methodology follows the CHEERS 2022 (Husereau et al., 2022) standards of reporting. We do not perform a full economic evaluation.We present cost-per-effect in two complementary forms. The primary metric is the cost per standardized mean difference (SMD) point reduction, which is comparable across studies that use different depression instruments. The secondary metric is cost per remission (defined as PHQ-9 < 5 at primary time-point, or the instrument-specific equivalent defined by each study). Studies that do not report effect sizes or remission rates contribute only to per-client cost tabulations. We tabulate costs alongside the Phase 3b cost figures (Alagarajah & Fraker, 2026) using the same columns, so the Strongminds team can read across internal and external evidence in a single view. 4.5.4 Triangulation matrixWe produce a 3-column triangulation matrix for each of the nine headline RQs that drive MVP design (RQ5, RQ6, RQ7, RQ8, RQ9, RQ12, RQ13, RQ14, RQ16). We include RQ8 as a headline RQ because the question of whether low-intensity interventions deliver short-term symptom relief without sustained benefit is decision-critical at the USD 1-per-client target. LMIC evidence is most direct, and SSA evidence is most direct for context-sensitive RQs. HIC-dominated reviews are most direct for the dose and SSI questions. Precision (qualitative judgement) We do not compute CIs around pooled estimates because we do not pool effect sizes. We do not apply the full GRADE framework, which is disproportionate to the scope of this rapid review, but the lite version makes confidence judgements auditable.4.5.6 Publication-bias considerationsAs we do not produce pooled estimates, we do not produce funnel plots or run Egger-type. For each RQ supported by more than or equal to 10 studies, the synthesis text includes a brief qualitative discussion of: (a) the balance of registered-trial versus grey-literature evidence; (b) whether non-significant results appear systematically under-represented relative to registry entries found during hand-search of International Clinical Trials Registry Platform (ICTRP) and ClinicalTrials.gov; and (c) the geographic and funder distribution of included studies. RQs supported by fewer than 10 studies carry an explicit “publication-bias risk not assessable” flag in the GRADE-lite domain narrative.4.6 Quality assuranceWe export all extraction sheets from EPPI-Reviewer weekly and version-control them in a Git repository.We record and version the LLM prompts, model versions, and seed configurations alongside the extraction data, so the screening and extraction workflow remains reproducible. A reviewer from the core team performs a 20% random audit of high-confidence categorical extractions and a 10% audit of high-confidence exclusion decisions at the end of weeks 3 and 6. The core team verifies 100% of quantitative fields used in synthesis against source PDFs.5. LimitationsThe following limitations are inherent to this rapid evidence mapping approach and should be considered when interpreting the findings.Rapid-review methods: To support timely decision-making, screening, extraction, and synthesis are conducted using streamlined rapid-review procedures, with several stages undertaken primarily by a single reviewer. Although calibration, verification, and audit checks are used to improve consistency, errors, omissions, or missed studies may remain.Potential conflict of interest: The review is funded by the organization developing the intervention under consideration. While procedural safeguards promote transparency and consistency, the interpretation of the evidence remains vulnerable to bias.Single screening throughout. Mitigated by LLM second-screening and adjudication, but does not equal dual independent human screening.English-only. Excludes Francophone West Africa, Lusophone Africa, and Latin American Spanish/Portuguese literature.Digital-only interventions excluded. A growing literature that may bear on long-term ULCM strategy but is out of scope for the current MVP.Specialist-delivered evidence admitted only for the dose questions. Reviews of specialist-delivered interventions enter only for the dose, single-session, and stepped-care questions (RQ7, RQ8, RQ9, RQ12, RQ14), where they are the dominant evidence base; they are not used for task-shifting or implementation claims.Review-sourced primary evidence; reviews not exhaustively re-searched. Both streams reach primary studies only through their entry-point systematic reviews (Stream 1: Barbui et al. (2020) - anchored reviews plus the post-2019 update; Stream 2: seed reviews plus one-hop citation chasing); primary studies not captured by any in-scope review are not retrieved. Primary studies are extracted for effect, dose, and design details, but are not individually risk-of-bias appraised; the AMSTAR-2 band of the sourcing review is applied to them. Primary-study drill-down is further bounded to the nine headline RQs plus RQ10 (Section 4.1.2); the remaining questions are answered at the review level, so their primary-study detail is read at the level reported in the contributing reviews.Two-stream design dependencies. Questions inside Barbui et al. (2020)’s scope are answered by a Barbui et al. (2020) - anchored search with drill-down to primary studies (Stream 1); questions outside it are answered from named seed reviews expanded by one-hop citation chasing (Stream 2). The main costs are: (i) review-lag and review-mediated coverage — primary studies enter only via a sourcing review, so very recent trials (the 2024–2026 window) and trials missed by all in-scope reviews may be under-represented; (ii) seed-selection dependence — Stream 2 coverage is conditioned on the choice of seed reviews and the reach of one-hop citation chasing, which may miss a relevant review not linked to any seed; and (iii) Barbui et al. (2020)’s inherited design choices for Stream 1, notably its broad population frame (which we narrow to adult CMD) and its May 2019 search end-date (filled by the update search). The exhaustiveness of each stream for each RQ will be reported in the synthesis (number of contributing reviews and primary studies; date range; AMSTAR-2 confidence band; stream of origin).Adolescents excluded. Findings should not be assumed transferable to under-18 populations.Schleider 2025 umbrella is youth-skewed. Adult transferability is an open question addressed within the review (RQ7, RQ9), not a framing assumption.Phase 3 directional signals are pre-registered as comparators in the triangulation matrix, not as hypotheses to be confirmed. The reviewer team will declare any case in which a Phase 3 finding (e.g., “two sessions as a possible minimum effective dose”) aligns with the external evidence, but will not adjust inclusion or extraction decisions on this basis.Internal evidence is shallow. Treated as triangulation only; never pooled with external evidence.No meta-analysis. Synthesis is narrative with targeted descriptive effect-size tabulation.SSA-weighting introduces evidentiary asymmetry. SSA review evidence is weighted higher for context-sensitive questions (RQ1, RQ4, RQ13), while the dose / SSI / stepped-care questions rely on HIC-dominated reviews; this asymmetry is applied transparently at synthesis, not through a tier system.Cost harmonization uses CPI + market FX, not PPP. This matches the donor-budget framing of the GLT decision but reduces comparability with the cost-effectiveness literature, which uses international dollars; the choice is noted in the synthesis section.DeclarationsFunding and commissioning. What are the primary biological, psychosocial, economic, and contextual drivers of depression in the target population group (adults with depression), as reported in existing research on similar contexts (e.g., LMICs with comparable demographic and socioeconomic profiles)?RQ2. Which low-intensity, task-shifted, group-based depression interventions have been employed in LMIC and comparable settings in high-income settings to address these drivers, and what evidence exists regarding their theoretical rationale, feasibility, and short/medium/long term clinical outcomes?RQ3. Which clinical, delivery, and cost-relevant parameters most strongly determine the scalability of low-intensity, task-shifted, group-based depression interventions in LMIC and comparable settings?RQ4. What levels of facilitator training, supervision, and clinical background are associated with acceptable outcomes in scalable group-based depression models in LMICs?RQ5. Among low-intensity, task-shifted, group-based depression interventions in LMIC and comparable settings, which therapeutic components (e.g., psychoeducation, problem-solving, peer support) are consistently associated with clinically meaningful symptom improvement?RQ6. Among low-intensity, task-shifted, group-based depression interventions in LMIC and comparable settings, which commonly included components show limited or inconsistent contribution to outcomes and can therefore be excluded or deprioritized in an Ultra-Low-Cost Model without materially reducing expected clinical effect?RQ7. What is the minimum viable dose (session length × number of sessions) for short-term depression symptom reduction in low-resource, task-shifted settings, stratified by baseline severity at enrolment (mild, moderate, severe), and at what point does further simplification reduce effectiveness?RQ8. Do low-intensity interventions seem to only work in the short term but not in the longer term? Do they work through different mechanisms — e.g., provide immediate symptom relief but not build skills to address future challenges?RQ9. Under what conditions — including population characteristics, baseline depression severity, psychiatric history, and delivery format — are single-session interventions clinically appropriate compared to brief multi-session models, and what evidence supports tailoring intervention dose or format according to baseline severity in low-intensity care settings?RQ10. What group size ranges are supported by the literature for group-based depression care without compromising clinical outcomes, participant engagement, or intervention fidelity, and where are the upper thresholds beyond which effectiveness or feasibility begins to decline?RQ11. What is the evidence on spillover effects of brief or light-touch interventions (e.g., psychoeducation, single-session interventions) delivered to general populations, including individuals without depression?RQ12. What is the optimal design of a stepped care model for depression, with a brief/single-session intervention as the first step and a 6-week intervention as the second, including whether allocation should be universal or triaged (e.g., using PHQ-9 thresholds)?RQ13. What level of clinical effectiveness can be achieved through non-specialist delivery models (e.g., lay-led, peer-led, or self-guided), and which HCD-informed design features are critical to maintaining outcomes while minimizing cost and complexity?RQ14. How does the therapeutic model (e.g., SSI vs MSI, active ingredients, session structure) influence the trajectory of clinical impact over time, including risk of short-term gains without sustained benefit?RQ15. How do different therapeutic design choices (e.g., session length, group format, facilitator role, interactivity) influence participant engagement, adherence, and completion rates?RQ16. What are the key cost drivers, indicative cost-per-participant ranges, and relative cost differences across low-intensity intervention models (e.g., SSI, MSI, stepped care), and what cost–effectiveness trade-offs are associated with variations in core design parameters (e.g., dose, group size, facilitator model)?RQ 17. What safety monitoring practices, adverse event procedures, and referral pathway designs have been used in lay-delivered brief psychological interventions in low-resource settings, and what evidence exists for their adequacy?RQ 18. What is the evidence on the validity and reliability of psychometric depression measurement tools in LMICs?Appendix B: Detailed screening decisionsThis appendix records worked examples for borderline screening decisions. It is populated during weeks 1–2 of the milestone as live decisions accrue. Two pre-specified worked examples below define how recurring boundary cases are handled.B.1 Review with a mixed adult / adolescent population and no adult subgroupScenario. A meta-analysis of lay-delivered group IPT pool trials among participants aged 15–24 reports a single pooled effect, without an adult (≥ 18) subgroup estimate.Decision rule. EXCLUDE at full-text under criterion P. The record is tagged excl_P_no_adult_subgroup and noted in the deviation register. If the review reports an adult-subgroup or adult-stratum estimate (e.g., in a forest plot or supplementary table), it is reinstated and only the adult stratum is extracted.B.2 Review of perinatal-depression interventionsScenario. A systematic review and meta-analysis of community-health-worker- delivered psychological interventions for antenatal and postnatal women meeting an EPDS threshold.Decision rule. INCLUDE under criterion P (perinatal samples are eligible). Record is tagged pop_perinatal for stratified synthesis. The intervention is in scope (lay-delivered structured psychological intervention), the outcome is in scope (depression symptom change on a validated instrument), and the unit is a systematic review.B.3 Primary study reached outside any in-scope reviewScenario. A standalone program evaluation of Friendship Bench in Zimbabwe reports PHQ-9 mean change from baseline to 6 weeks for 250 enrolled participants, retrieved directly and not cited by any in-scope systematic review.Decision rule. If the study is single-arm or uncontrolled, EXCLUDE under criterion C/S (no eligible comparator). If it is a controlled trial meeting PICOS, it is eligible only when it is sourced from an in-scope systematic review (Stream 1): primary studies enter the corpus through the reviews that contain them, not via free-standing retrieval. A controlled, in-PICOS study not linked to any in-scope review is tagged excl_S_not_review_sourced. The record may still be retrieved as internal context for RQ1 and RQ15 if it is published by StrongMinds or Friendship Bench under the internal evidence stream; it does not enter the external corpus or contribute to vote-counting.B.4 Meta-regression of specialist-delivered HIC dose dataScenario. A meta-regression of UK NHS Talking Therapies (IAPT) data on session number and outcome in depression, where clinical-psychologist-supervised practitioners deliver most therapy.Decision rule. INCLUDE: a meta-regression is a review-level synthesis, so it satisfies criterion S, and specialist-delivered HIC dose evidence is eligible for the dose questions (Stream 2). Tagged stream2 and dose_evidence. Contributes to RQ7, RQ8, RQ9 synthesis only; SSA-context claims are not drawn from it.Appendix C: Search stringsThis appendix records the planned, deployable search strings used to populate EPPI-Reviewer.
